Now, there's an important question here: if C-14 has a half-life of 5730 years and the earth is 4.5 billion years old, why do we still see any C-14? The answer is that it gets produced by cosmic rays in the upper atmosphere.

Cosmic rays are super high energy particles that fly in from way out in space- their origin is mysterious and the explanation for why they can sometimes have such extraordinarily high energies is still an open problem in physics. These particles collide with other particles and atoms in the upper atmosphere, producing showers of yet more particles, some of which are neutrons. High-energy neutrons can interact with nitrogen in the following way:

N-14 + n -> C-14 + p + e

Importantly, these neutrons will not penetrate the ground, meaning that only carbon in the atmosphere can be converted into C-14. Carbon that has been buried in the form of hydrocarbons will simply decay, so by the time we take it back out of the ground it has effectively zero C-14.
