Even among the rationalist community, people who pride themselves on constructing accurate world models fall prey to this tendency. Take, for example, Scott Alexander's amazing essay Meditations on Moloch. In it, he attempts to describe the ways in which systems of interacting components can, without any intent on the part of the components themselves, lead to large scale patterns of predictable behavior (think Prisoner's Dillema or Tragedy of the Commons, but with an almost all-consuming scope). The central metaphor of the piece is Moloch, a demon taken from Allen Ginsberg's poem Howl. He acts as the personification of impersonal force. Our desire for anthropomorphism is so strong that even the essence of inhuman patterns gets turned into an agent.

It's no coincidence that both Scott and Ginsberg use a demon as their metaphor- gods and other supernatural creatures carry the two qualities that we are designed to look for. They are both human and eternal. They take some pattern that clearly exists in the world and allow a way to predict the outcome. These models for the world are natural- even a physicist or a chemist who knows better will default to saying that water "wants" to get to the bottom of a hill or that an electron "wants" to relax to its ground state. Why? Because even if you explicitly know a better model for these things, the fact that you are human, with a set of hardware equipped for social modelling, means that you still default to action- or goal-based models because they're easier to compute. Since we cannot resist the temptation of personification, we must simply embrace it and find ways to harness that into the most effective model. Hence the beauty of frameworks like the 2nd law of thermodynamics (systems want to maximize their energy subject to some constraint) and Lagrangian mechanics (systems want to minimize their action subject to some constraint).




The extreme cases of personality change in trance, hypnosis, Mask work, meditation, drug use, etc. should be considered not fringe examples of personality gone haywire but rather as insights into what personality really is. It's a lot like optical illusions. One way to view them is as something gone wrong- there's a decoupling between perceived and actual reality. Another way to view them is as looking directly at how perception works. The latter view is much more productive. When finding a pattern, you can't just look at what fits the pattern, you have to look at what doesn't fit pattern.

"Each pattern is a generic solution to some system of forces in the world. But the forces are never quite the same.
